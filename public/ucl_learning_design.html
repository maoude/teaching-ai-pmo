Learning Objectives

Mapped to Bloom's Taxonomy — knowledge, application, and synthesis levels

By the end of this session, students will be able to…
  1. Understand Explain the project management cycle from initiation to closing and identify the PMO's role at each phase.
  2. Apply Build a deliverable-oriented Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) for a realistic software project.
  3. Apply Convert a WBS into tasks, dependencies, milestones, and responsibilities using a schedule table.
  4. Analyze Interpret project tracking indicators (PV, EV, AC, SV, CV, SPI, CPI) from a live dashboard.
  5. Analyze Diagnose project health problems from real dashboard data and identify root causes.
  6. Evaluate Optimize a project schedule under time and budget constraints using compression techniques.
  7. Create Propose corrective actions for a late, over-budget project and present a professional PMO recovery briefing.
  8. Create Communicate project status and decisions clearly to a sponsor under uncertainty and pressure.

Pedagogical Strategy

Six design principles behind the instructional choices in this module

1. Authentic Scenario Learning

The Smart Campus Mobile App is a realistic, recognisable project. Students inhabit a genuine PMO context rather than abstract theory, which increases motivation and transfer. Every tool and decision mirrors real professional practice.

2. Progressive Complexity

Activities escalate in cognitive demand: from receiving a brief (Acquisition) → analysing stakeholders (Inquiry) → building deliverables (Production) → diagnosing failures (Analyze) → proposing recovery (Create). Each level builds on the previous.

3. AI as Cognitive Tool, Not Shortcut

AI tools (NotebookLM, Claude, Gamma, Canva) are embedded as productivity amplifiers. Students use AI to draft, check, and visualise — not to bypass thinking. The instructor models responsible AI use throughout.

4. Formative Feedback Loops

Polls, quizzes, and class debriefs after each segment give immediate feedback. Students don't wait until the exam to know if their mental model is correct. This is especially critical for quantitative concepts like EV and CPI.

5. Social & Collaborative Learning

Groups work as PMO teams throughout: negotiating scope, dividing WBS branches, debating recovery strategies. The final briefing simulates a real sponsor meeting. Social pressure and peer accountability drive engagement.

6. Gamification for Engagement

Five quest levels, six badge types, immediate point rewards, and a visible progression system maintain energy across 3 hours. The gamified structure turns a long session into a series of achievable challenges rather than an endurance exercise.

AI Integration Strategy

Each tool serves a specific instructional purpose — AI augments, not replaces, human thinking

📓 NotebookLM

Source-grounded concept summaries from uploaded course notes. Used in Segment 2 for stakeholder and charter concepts.

💬 Claude / ChatGPT / Gemini

Scenario writing, crisis card generation, PMO feedback prompts. Used by instructor for content prep and by students during crisis simulation.

🎨 Gamma

First-draft slide generation from the session outline. Instructor refines for accuracy and local context.

🖼 Canva / Napkin.ai

Visual WBS diagrams, badge design, printable worksheets. Students use in Segment 3 to render their WBS visually.

🌀 Genially

Branching interactive simulation for opening scenario and crisis decision. Provides immediate narrative feedback based on student choice.

📊 Google Sheets / Excel

Live schedule table (Segment 4) and EV dashboard (Segment 5). Students work with real formulas, not static images.

🧪 Quizizz / MagicSchool

Formative quiz after Dashboard Detective segment. Instant scoring reveals conceptual gaps before the Crisis simulation.

📢 Mentimeter / Wooclap

Live audience polls at session open and close. Creates visibility into collective understanding and gives the instructor real-time feedback.

Pedagogical Rationale

Why this design makes the 3-hour session effective — the reasoning behind each structural choice

Why open with a scenario and a poll?

A poll immediately activates prior knowledge and surfaces misconceptions before instruction begins. The scenario provides a compelling hook that sustains motivation throughout the session. Students who vote "wrong" feel safe to revise — the debrief normalises error as a learning event rather than a failure.

Why keep Acquisition to 12.5% (22.5 minutes)?

Research on cognitive load (Sweller, 1988; Mayer, 2009) shows that passive listening degrades after 10–15 minutes. Keeping direct instruction short and embedding it immediately before a practice task ensures concepts are applied while they are fresh. Students learn PMO by doing, not by listening.

Why allocate 38.8% to Practice + Collaboration combined?

The module targets engineering and management students who will face real projects. Skill transfer requires repeated practice in context. Collaboration mirrors the actual PMO team environment — no real PM works alone. The ratio ensures students spend more time acting like PMOs than observing one.

Why end with a crisis rather than a quiz?

A final high-stakes, ambiguous scenario (the integration failure) forces synthesis: students must use everything they built — charter, WBS, schedule, dashboard — to propose a coherent response. This mirrors Bloom's highest level (Create + Evaluate) and provides a memorable, emotional anchor for long-term retention. The crisis is the exam disguised as a game.

Why use gamification across all 5 segments?

A 3-hour session without structured engagement typically loses students after 90 minutes. Quest progression, badges, and immediate feedback maintain intrinsic motivation by giving students a visible sense of achievement at every stage. The badge system also externalises competence — students leave with something to show, not just notes to read later.

Why integrate AI tools rather than using slides alone?

Students entering the workforce will use AI. Modelling responsible AI use — NotebookLM to summarise theory, Claude to generate crisis cards, Genially to simulate decisions — teaches the skill of tool selection alongside the subject matter. It also demonstrates that AI produces a first draft, not a final answer.
